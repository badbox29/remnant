Terminology

Remnant organizes your writing into four nested levels. Here's what each one means and how they fit together.

Library

Everything you keep in Remnant. The Library holds every Corpus you've created — it's the whole of your collection, not a thing you organize directly.

Corpus

A major body of work — a project, an area of your life, a broad subject. The top-level grouping inside your Library. A Corpus holds one or more Scrolls.

Scroll

A section within a Corpus — something narrower than the whole body of work, but still a grouping rather than a single piece of writing. A Scroll holds one or more Remnants.

Remnant

A single saved piece of writing — what most tools would call a "note." The thing you actually read and write, day to day.

Cipher

A Remnant's locked sibling. A Cipher holds real content, lives in your Library the same way, and opens in a tab the same way — but its body is encrypted with a passphrase only you know, and even after you've unlocked it, the page never just sits there in plain view. The text stays disguised at rest, with only a small window of real content following your cursor (or your finger, just above the touch point) as you read — so someone glancing at your screen over your shoulder sees gibberish, not your words. An "Illuminate" option exists for when you need to read or edit the whole thing at once, with a clear visual warning while it's active.

Library → Corpus → Scroll → Remnant

Any Remnant can also be left loose — outside any Scroll — and you'll find it waiting in Loose Remnants at the bottom of the Library until you decide where it belongs.

New Cipher

There is no way to recover a Cipher if you forget its passphrase.

The passphrase is never stored anywhere — not on this device, not in sync, not by Remnant in any form. Store it somewhere secure outside this app (a password manager, written down and kept safe) before you rely on it. The Cipher's title stays visible in your Library; only its contents are protected.

Illuminate Cipher

This will display the Cipher in full, with no masking. Anyone able to see your screen will be able to read it. Useful for editing — but make sure you're somewhere private first.
